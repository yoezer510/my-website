Nature & Landscape of Bhutan

Bhutan is a kingdom blessed with pristine landscapes and untouched natural beauty. Nestled in the Himalayas, the country is known for its high mountains, lush valleys, and rich biodiversity. Almost 70% of Bhutan’s land is covered in forests, making it one of the greenest nations in the world.

Himalayan Mountains

The Eastern Himalayas dominate Bhutan’s terrain, with peaks such as Gangkhar Puensum, the world’s highest unclimbed mountain. Mountains not only provide breathtaking scenery but also influence climate, wildlife, and local spiritual beliefs.

Valleys

Bhutan is home to lush valleys like Paro, Thimphu, and Punakha. These valleys are rich in fertile land, colorful flora, and traditional villages. They are centers of culture, agriculture, and festivals, offering serene landscapes and vibrant community life.

Forests

Over 70% of Bhutan is covered in forests, ranging from subtropical to alpine. These forests are vital for biodiversity, housing rare species like the red panda and takin, and contribute to Bhutan’s commitment to environmental conservation and Gross National Happiness.

Rivers

Bhutan’s rivers, like the Punatsang Chhu and Wang Chhu, flow from the Himalayas through valleys, providing water for agriculture, hydropower, and recreation. They shape the landscape and sustain both wildlife and human communities.

Lakes

Glacial and high-altitude lakes such as Gangteng and Tshophu are mystical and serene. They are often sites of spiritual significance and attract both locals and tourists for their natural beauty.

Hot Springs

Known as “tsho” or “khola,” Bhutan’s natural hot springs are both therapeutic and culturally significant. Locals visit them for healing purposes, and many are set amidst stunning mountain scenery.

Wildlife

Bhutan is rich in biodiversity, hosting species like the takin, snow leopard, and red panda. Protected national parks and wildlife sanctuaries maintain this natural heritage while promoting eco-tourism.

Glaciers

Bhutan’s high mountains are home to numerous glaciers, which are the source of its rivers and streams. They play a crucial role in maintaining the ecosystem, providing fresh water, and shaping the landscape over centuries.
